# Seeded Rationalization Patterns by Enneagram Type

When generating the user's initial `patterns.md` during onboarding, use the patterns below for their confirmed type. These are hypotheses — label them as such. Present them to the user: "Based on your type, you may recognize these patterns. Which ring true? Which are wrong? What's missing?" Remove any the user rejects. Add any they identify that aren't listed.

Format for each pattern: what it sounds like, what's actually happening, how to respond.

---

## Type 1: The Reformer

**Core pattern: Perfection as avoidance.**

1. **The Standard Shield** — "I can't start until I have the right approach." Perfectionism disguised as preparation. Actually: fear of doing it wrong prevents doing it at all. Response: "What's a good-enough version you could do today?"

2. **The Righteous Exception** — "I need to fix this first, it's wrong." Correcting something urgent-feeling (but not urgent) displaces committed activities. Actually: the inner critic found something "wrong" and can't leave it alone. Response: "Is this your commitment for today, or is this your inner critic hijacking your schedule?"

3. **The Improvement Spiral** — "I'm almost there, I just need to refine this a little more." Nothing is ever good enough to stop. Actually: finishing means being judged, and imperfection is intolerable. Response: "What would 'done' look like? Not perfect — done."

4. **The Duty Override** — "I should do this. It's the responsible thing." An obligation (real or manufactured) displaces a personal goal. Actually: duty is prioritized over desire, then resented. Response: "Whose rule says you 'should'? Is that rule serving you or running you?"

5. **The Self-Punishment Cycle** — "I already broke my commitment, so the day is ruined." One slip becomes permission to abandon everything. Actually: the inner critic turns a small failure into a total one. Response: "One broken commitment is data. Abandoning the rest is a choice. What can you still keep today?"

## Type 2: The Helper

**Core pattern: Others' needs as escape from own needs.**

1. **The Needed Elsewhere** — "They really need my help right now." Helping someone else displaces a personal commitment. Actually: being needed feels more important (and safer) than showing up for yourself. Response: "What would happen if you said no? Would they survive? Would you?"

2. **The Relationship Justification** — "We're spending quality time together." Social activity that feels relational but avoids the committed personal goal. Actually: being around people is the comfort zone. Response: "Is this the relationship investment you planned, or is this avoiding the thing you planned to do alone?"

3. **The Guilt Deflection** — "I feel so guilty about [other person], I can't focus." Guilt about someone else becomes a reason not to do personal work. Actually: focusing on others' feelings avoids confronting your own goals. Response: "You're feeling guilty about them. What would they say if you told them you skipped your own goal because of it?"

4. **The Invisible Needs** — "I'm fine, everyone else needs more." Minimizing personal needs as less important. Actually: acknowledging your own needs feels selfish, so they get perpetually deferred. Response: "When was the last time you put your own need first? Not after everyone else's — first."

5. **The Overextension Collapse** — "I'm too exhausted to do my thing." Genuinely drained because everything was given to others. Actually: the exhaustion is real but manufactured by not setting boundaries. Response: "You're exhausted because you said yes to everything else first. What would your week look like if your commitments to yourself were scheduled before your commitments to others?"

6. **The Indispensability Trap** — "They can't do it without me." Believing your involvement is uniquely necessary. Actually: this is pride disguised as service — the need to be needed is the driver, not the genuine necessity of your help. Response: "What would actually happen if you weren't available? Would they figure it out? Is your involvement serving them or serving your need to be essential?"

## Type 3: The Achiever

**Core pattern: Productive busyness as identity protection.**

1. **The Efficiency Trap** — "I can do both, I'll fit it in later." Overestimating capacity because admitting limits feels like failure. Actually: "later" never comes because the schedule keeps filling. Response: "When is 'later'? Give me a time. If you can't name a time, it's not happening."

2. **The Metric Substitution** — "I hit my other goals, so today was good." Counting wins in areas that are easy to dismiss the one that didn't get attention. Actually: there's always a scoreboard going up. The question is which scoreboards aren't. Response: "Which needle didn't move? That's the one we're talking about."

3. **The Image Management** — "People are counting on me to deliver this." Real-sounding external pressure that justifies overwork. Actually: the pressure is about maintaining the image of someone who always delivers. Response: "Who specifically asked for this by today? Or is this the standard you're imposing on yourself?"

4. **The Pivot to Progress** — "This isn't working, let me try a different approach." Abandoning a commitment disguised as strategic adaptation. Actually: if it's not producing visible results fast, the impulse is to switch to something that will. Response: "Is this a real strategy change, or are you quitting because it's not working fast enough?"

5. **The Rest-Is-Lazy Belief** — "I'll rest when I've earned it." Rest and non-productive time feel like failure. Actually: rest threatens the identity of someone who is always performing. Response: "What would you have to accomplish before rest felt earned? Is that bar achievable today, or does it move every time you approach it?"

6. **The Decoupled Self** — "I know who I am outside of work." Said with confidence, but the calendar tells a different story. Actually: there's a genuine belief in having a life outside achievement because the self-image requires it. Response: "Show me on this week's log where non-achievement time happened. Not rest-to-perform-better — time that had no productive purpose."

## Type 4: The Individualist

**Core pattern: Emotional truth as avoidance of mundane action.**

1. **The Conditions Aren't Right** — "I'm not in the right headspace." Waiting for emotional alignment before acting. Actually: the "right mood" is a moving target that may never arrive. Response: "When in the last month were you in the right headspace? How many commitments would you have kept if you only acted when conditions were right?"

2. **The Depth Dismissal** — "That goal feels superficial now." A previously meaningful goal gets reframed as beneath them. Actually: shifting emotional landscape makes yesterday's commitment feel inauthentic today. Response: "It mattered when you wrote it down. What changed — the goal, or your mood?"

3. **The Authenticity Shield** — "I can't force it, it has to feel genuine." Using authenticity as a reason to not do things that feel routine. Actually: discipline feels inauthentic, so it gets rejected. Response: "Does brushing your teeth feel genuine? Some things that matter don't need to feel inspired."

4. **The Uniqueness Exemption** — "That approach works for other people, not for me." Rejecting conventional methods because they don't feel special enough. Actually: using the same approach as everyone else threatens the sense of uniqueness. Response: "What if ordinary methods with consistent execution is the most unusual thing you could do?"

5. **The Suffering-As-Depth** — "At least I feel the weight of this. That means something." Treating the pain of not following through as meaningful in itself. Actually: feeling bad about not acting is being confused with growth. Response: "Feeling bad about it is not the same as doing something about it. Which one happened this week?"

6. **The Missing Piece** — "Other people seem to just... do this naturally. I can't." Believing others have something fundamentally available to them that you lack — discipline, ease, normalcy. Actually: this is envy reframed as deficiency. The belief that you're constitutionally different makes effort feel pointless. Response: "What specifically do you think they have that you don't? Now — is that a fact or a story? What would you do differently if the story weren't true?"

## Type 5: The Investigator

**Core pattern: Preparation and depth as avoidance of engagement.**

1. **The Research Extension** — "I need to understand this better before I act." Infinite preparation that delays action indefinitely. Actually: acting feels costly because energy is guarded. More research feels productive without requiring commitment. Response: "What would you need to know before you'd feel ready? Write it down. Now — do you actually need all of that, or is this the infinite preparation loop?"

2. **The Energy Budget** — "I don't have the bandwidth today." Genuine experience of limited energy that conveniently protects against demands. Actually: the energy is real but the threshold for "too much" keeps lowering. Response: "What did you spend your energy on today? Was it your commitments, or was it what felt easier?"

3. **The Deep Dive Disappearance** — "I've been working on [fascinating tangent] for hours." Absorbed in something intellectually stimulating that is not the commitment. Actually: curiosity leads into rabbit holes and hours vanish. Response: "That sounds genuinely interesting. Was it on your plan for today?"

4. **The Observation Substitution** — "I've been thinking about this a lot." Thinking replaces doing. Analysis replaces action. Actually: thinking about something feels like progress on it. Response: "Thinking about it and doing it live in different columns. Which column has entries this week?"

5. **The Withdrawal as Processing** — "I need to be alone to process this." Retreating from engagement (especially interpersonal) under the guise of processing. Actually: withdrawal happens when demands feel overwhelming, and "processing" is an acceptable reason. Response: "How long do you need? Name a time you'll re-engage. If you can't name a time, this is withdrawal, not processing."

6. **The Competence Gate** — "I need to get better at this before I start." Requiring mastery before attempting something new. Actually: fear of being seen as incompetent. Practice in private forever is preferable to being a beginner in public. Response: "Everyone who's good at this was once terrible at it in public. What's the cost of one awkward session versus another month of not starting?"

## Type 6: The Loyalist

**Core pattern: Anxiety and worst-case thinking as avoidance of commitment.**

1. **The What-If Spiral** — "But what if it doesn't work out?" Generating worst-case scenarios that paralyze action. Actually: the anxiety is real, but the scenarios are a mechanism for staying safe by not committing. Response: "What's the actual worst case? Write it down. Now — could you survive that? Probably yes. What's the worst case of not starting?"

2. **The Authority Check** — "I should ask [someone] first." Seeking permission or validation before acting on their own goals. Actually: certainty is outsourced to trusted authorities to avoid the risk of trusting themselves. Response: "What would you decide if nobody else's opinion was available? That's probably the right answer."

3. **The Safety Net Delay** — "I need a backup plan before I start." Requiring complete contingency planning before any action. Actually: the backup plan is a way to feel safe, but it delays the thing that actually reduces risk — starting. Response: "You have a backup plan for the backup plan. At what point is the planning itself the avoidance?"

4. **The Loyalty Conflict** — "I can't do my thing because [person/group] needs me." Genuine loyalty to others that consistently overrides personal commitments. Actually: prioritizing oneself over the trusted inner circle feels like betrayal. Response: "Loyalty to others at the cost of your own goals — is that what the people you're loyal to actually want from you?"

5. **The Testing Pattern** — "Is this system really going to help? How do I know this works?" Questioning the process before committing to it. Actually: testing everything and everyone before trusting. Healthy skepticism becomes avoidance when it never resolves. Response: "You've been testing for [timeframe]. At what point do you commit to trying, knowing you can adjust?"

6. **The Counterphobic Leap** — "I'll just do it, what's the worst that could happen?" Rushing into action to prove the fear doesn't control them — but without preparation or structure. Actually: counterphobic Sixes charge toward fear rather than retreating from it, but the impulsive action is still fear-driven — it's fighting the anxiety instead of being paralyzed by it. Response: "That's courage, not a plan. What's your structure for this? Charging in without one is the flip side of not starting at all."

7. **The Independence Overcorrection** — "I don't need anyone's help with this." Refusing support or accountability to prove self-sufficiency. Actually: counterphobic Sixes distrust authority and resist dependence, but isolating from support removes the external structure they need most. Response: "Not needing help and not accepting help are different things. Which is this?"

## Type 7: The Enthusiast

**Core pattern: New and exciting as escape from committed and uncomfortable.**

1. **The Shiny Object** — "I just had an amazing idea for something new." Generating new projects/ideas when current commitments get boring or hard. Actually: novelty is the drug. New ideas feel like opportunity; following through feels like limitation. Response: "Write that idea down. Now — what were you supposed to be doing when it arrived?"

2. **The Positive Reframe** — "I didn't do X, but I did Y, which is actually better!" Reframing failure as a different kind of success. Actually: anything can be spun into a positive. This makes accountability nearly impossible unless the system doesn't accept the reframe. Response: "Y might be great. It wasn't the commitment. Did you do what you said you'd do? That's the question."

3. **The Experience Substitution** — "I'm doing something — I'm active, I'm engaged, I'm living!" General activity masquerading as committed activity. Actually: staying busy and stimulated feels like a full life but may not touch the goals that were set. Response: "Active is not the same as aligned. Were today's activities in service of your goals, or in service of stimulation?"

4. **The Boredom Escape** — "This is getting repetitive. I need to switch things up." Abandoning a commitment because it lost its novelty. Actually: the commitment didn't change. The threshold for repetition kicked in. Response: "Growth lives in the repetitions. The exciting part was starting. The valuable part is continuing. Which one are you choosing?"

5. **The FOMO Override** — "I can't miss this — it's a once-in-a-lifetime thing." An event, opportunity, or experience displaces a commitment because of perceived scarcity. Actually: everything feels once-in-a-lifetime because missing anything feels like a small death. Response: "Is this actually once-in-a-lifetime, or does it feel that way because saying no to an experience is the hardest thing for you?"

6. **The Future Fix** — "I'll be more disciplined next month / after this trip / when things settle down." Projecting change into a future that never arrives. Actually: the future always looks more disciplined because it's hypothetical. Response: "Describe what's different about next month. Specifically. If you can't name the structural change, it's the same month with a different name."

## Type 8: The Challenger

**Core pattern: Intensity and control as avoidance of vulnerability.**

1. **The Bulldozer** — "I'll just push through this. I don't need a plan." Brute-forcing through things without the structure that makes effort sustainable. Actually: personal strength is trusted more than systems. But pushing through without structure leads to burnout and collateral damage. Response: "You can push through anything once. Can you push through it every week for a year? That's what structure is for."

2. **The My-Way Override** — "Your suggestion doesn't work for me. I'll do it my way." Rejecting accountability structure because it feels like being controlled. Actually: resistance to any framework that wasn't self-originated. Response: "You designed this system. These are your goals. The structure isn't controlling you — it's serving the thing you said you wanted."

3. **The Vulnerability Dodge** — "I don't need to talk about how I feel about this. Just tell me what to do." Deflecting emotional content with action-orientation. Actually: vulnerability feels like weakness. Engagement with feelings feels like exposure. Response: "You asked for coaching, not just a task list. The thing you're avoiding talking about might be the thing that's in the way."

4. **The Intensity Justification** — "I go hard because that's what it takes." Using intensity and long hours as evidence of commitment rather than a pattern to question. Actually: excess in service of goals is respected. But intensity in one area often means devastation in others. Response: "Going hard at [X] — what's the casualty list? Which other areas are paying the tax for this intensity?"

5. **The Control Expansion** — "I need to handle this personally." Taking on more direct control when systems or people don't meet the standard. Actually: scope of control expands when things feel like they're slipping. This crowds out everything else. Response: "You're taking on more because you don't trust [person/system] to do it right. What would letting go of this actually cost? What does holding it cost?"

6. **The Denial of Impact** — "They're fine. I wasn't that harsh." Minimizing the effect of their intensity on others. Actually: Eights often don't register how their forcefulness lands — what feels like directness to them feels like a steamroller to others. This matters because broken relationships erode the external structure goals depend on. Response: "You say they're fine. Have you asked? What would they say if you did?"

## Type 9: The Peacemaker

**Core pattern: Numbing inertia disguised as contentment.**

1. **The Everything's Fine** — "Things are good. I don't really need to change anything." Contentment that's actually numbness. Actually: merging with the current state and losing touch with personal desires. "Fine" is often "asleep." Response: "When you wrote these goals, you wanted something different from what you have now. What changed — your desires, or your willingness to pursue them?"

2. **The Path of Least Resistance** — "I'll just go along with what everyone else wants." Defaulting to others' agendas to avoid asserting their own. Actually: personal priorities are suppressed to maintain harmony. Response: "What would YOU have done today if nobody else's preference was a factor? That's the commitment you keep avoiding."

3. **The Merge** — "We were going to exercise, but then [partner/friend] wanted to [other thing]." Absorbing someone else's plan and abandoning their own without noticing. Actually: merging with others happens so seamlessly that personal wants get lost. Response: "At what point did your plan become their plan? Can you identify the moment you let go of what you wanted?"

4. **The Comfortable Routine** — "I'll get to it eventually. There's no rush." No urgency because the status quo is comfortable. Actually: inaction feels peaceful rather than urgent. Response: "How long has 'eventually' been the timeline? At what point does 'no rush' become 'never'?"

5. **The Non-Decision** — "I haven't decided yet." Indefinite postponement of commitment framed as ongoing deliberation. Actually: not deciding IS deciding — it's deciding to stay where you are. Response: "Not choosing is choosing the status quo. Is that what you want, or is it what's easiest?"

6. **The Self-Erasure** — "It doesn't matter that much to me." Minimizing the importance of their own goals. Actually: personal wants are habitually diminished. If it didn't matter, it wouldn't have been written down. Response: "You wrote this goal down. Something in you wanted it enough to name it. What happens if you take that part of yourself seriously?"
