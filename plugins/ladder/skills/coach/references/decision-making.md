# Decision-Making

Use when the user is facing a decision — big or small — and is stuck in analysis paralysis, afraid of choosing wrong, overwhelmed by options, or needs a framework for thinking clearly under uncertainty.

---

## Thinking in Bets (Annie Duke)

Every decision is a bet on an uncertain future. The quality of a decision is NOT determined by its outcome.

**Resulting:** The error of judging a decision by its outcome. A good decision with a bad outcome was still a good decision. A bad decision with a good outcome was still a bad decision. If you judge decisions by outcomes, you'll learn the wrong lessons.

**The framework:**
- What are the possible outcomes?
- What's the probability of each?
- What's the magnitude of each (how good or how bad)?
- Given those probabilities and magnitudes, what's the best bet?

**The 10-10-10 rule:** How will you feel about this decision in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? Decisions that feel massive in 10 minutes often feel trivial in 10 months.

**Probabilistic thinking:** Replace "I'm sure" with "I'm 70% confident." This small shift opens the door to updating your beliefs when new evidence arrives.

**Apply when:** the user is stuck because they're afraid of making the "wrong" choice, or has made a decision that turned out badly and is beating themselves up. Help them separate decision quality from outcome quality.

## Thinking, Fast and Slow — Applied to Decisions (Daniel Kahneman)

Most bad decisions come from System 1 (fast, intuitive, automatic) when System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical) should have been engaged — or from cognitive biases that distort perception.

**Key biases that sabotage decisions:**

- **Loss aversion:** Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. This makes people irrationally risk-averse when they should act, and irrationally risk-seeking when trying to avoid a certain loss.

- **Anchoring:** The first number you hear disproportionately influences your estimate. In negotiations, in pricing, in expectations — the anchor sets the range.

- **Availability bias:** You overweight information that comes to mind easily (recent, vivid, emotional) and underweight information that requires effort to retrieve.

- **Sunk cost fallacy:** Continuing to invest in something because of what you've already invested, not because of future returns. "I've already put 3 years into this" is not a reason to stay.

- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking information that confirms what you already believe and ignoring information that contradicts it.

**The practice:** Before any major decision, ask: "Which bias might be operating here?" Name it. Then deliberately seek the opposite perspective.

**Apply when:** the user is making a decision and you can hear a bias in their reasoning. Name the bias specifically and gently — not to invalidate their thinking, but to help them see the distortion.

## Fear-Setting for Decisions (Tim Ferriss)

When the decision involves risk and the fear is blocking action.

**The process:**

**Define:** What is the absolute worst case if you make this choice? Write it down in specific, concrete terms.

**Prevent:** What could you do to reduce the probability of the worst case? What preparation would help?

**Repair:** If the worst case happens, what would recovery look like? How long would it take? What resources would you need?

**Cost of inaction:** What does your life look like in 6 months, 1 year, 5 years if you do NOT make this choice? What's the cost of the default path?

**The insight:** People overestimate the cost of action and underestimate the cost of inaction. The status quo has a price — it's just less visible because it's spread over time.

**Apply when:** the user knows what they want to do but is paralyzed by worst-case thinking. Fear-setting makes the fear concrete and often reveals it's smaller than imagined.

## The Psychology of Money — Applied to Decisions (Morgan Housel)

Every financial decision (and many life decisions) is shaped by your personal history with money and risk, not by rational calculation.

**Key principles for decisions:**

- **Reasonable > Rational:** The mathematically optimal choice is not always the best choice. A slightly suboptimal choice you can live with beats an optimal choice that keeps you up at night.

- **Room for error:** The most important part of any plan is planning for the plan not going according to plan. Build margins. Assume surprises.

- **The role of luck and risk:** Every outcome is a combination of decisions, luck, and risk. Be careful attributing success entirely to skill and failure entirely to personal deficiency.

- **Tail events:** A small number of events drive a disproportionate share of outcomes. You don't need to be right on every decision — you need to survive the losses and be positioned for the rare big wins.

**Apply when:** the user is agonizing over optimization when "good enough" would serve them better, or is attributing too much to their own agency (in success or failure) and ignoring the role of randomness.
