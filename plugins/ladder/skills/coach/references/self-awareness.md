# Self-Awareness

Use when the user is trying to understand themselves better — why they do what they do, what's driving a pattern, what they're avoiding, or where their blind spots are.

---

## Shame Resilience Theory (Brene Brown)

Shame is the fear of disconnection — the belief that something about us makes us unworthy of belonging. It drives hiding, people-pleasing, perfectionism, and numbing.

**Shame vs. Guilt:**
- **Guilt:** "I did something bad." (Adaptive — leads to change.)
- **Shame:** "I am bad." (Maladaptive — leads to hiding, rage, or disconnection.)

**The Shame Resilience Process:**
1. Recognize shame when it's happening (physical cues: chest tightness, heat, shrinking)
2. Identify the trigger — what expectation or identity is being threatened?
3. Reach out — share the experience with someone who has earned the right to hear it
4. Speak the shame — naming it reduces its power

**Apply when:** the user is beating themselves up, spiraling in self-criticism, avoiding something because of fear of judgment, or unable to distinguish between "I made a mistake" and "I'm a failure."

## Vulnerability Armor (Brene Brown)

We develop armor to protect against vulnerability. The armor worked once but now blocks connection and growth.

**Common armor types:**
- **Foreboding joy:** Rehearsing tragedy when things are going well ("waiting for the other shoe to drop")
- **Perfectionism:** Using flawlessness as a shield against criticism
- **Numbing:** Dulling emotions to avoid discomfort (but you can't selectively numb — it kills joy too)
- **Viking or Victim:** Either dominate or collapse; no middle ground

**The antidote:** Practice gratitude in moments of joy instead of bracing. Name the armor when you notice it. Choose to be seen anyway.

**Apply when:** the user describes self-protective patterns that are now causing problems — can't celebrate wins, keeps people at arm's length, uses busyness or substances to avoid feeling, oscillates between overconfidence and collapse.

## The Self-Awareness Onion (Mark Manson)

Self-awareness has layers. Most people stop at the first layer and think they're done.

**Layer 1 — What:** "I feel angry." (Most people can get here.)
**Layer 2 — Why:** "I feel angry because I feel disrespected." (Harder. Requires honest introspection.)
**Layer 3 — Values:** "Being disrespected triggers me because I value being seen as competent, and that value comes from growing up in an environment where competence was the only safe form of worth." (Most people never get here.)

**The key insight:** Layer 1 awareness without Layer 3 awareness creates people who can name their feelings but can't change their patterns. The deepest layer — understanding what values drive the emotions — is where real change happens.

**Apply when:** the user can describe what they're feeling but keeps ending up in the same patterns, or when they're stuck on "I know I shouldn't feel this way but I do."

## Self-Sabotage as Subconscious Safety (Brianna Wiest)

Self-sabotage is not weakness or broken willpower. It's the subconscious mind protecting you from something it perceives as threatening — even when your conscious mind wants it.

**The mechanism:** Your subconscious builds an identity around what's familiar. Growth requires becoming someone unfamiliar, which triggers a threat response. The sabotage is an attempt to return to the known self.

**Common forms:**
- Procrastinating on the thing you care about most (success would change your identity)
- Starting fights when a relationship gets close (intimacy feels unsafe)
- Getting sick, tired, or overwhelmed right before a breakthrough
- Choosing chaos over calm because calm feels suspicious

**The work:** Notice the sabotage without judging it. Ask: "What is the part of me that's doing this trying to protect me from?" Then: "Is that threat real, or is it a memory?"

**Apply when:** the user keeps undermining their own goals in ways that seem irrational, or describes patterns where they get close to what they want and then blow it up.

## The Pain Body (Eckhart Tolle)

The pain body is accumulated emotional pain from the past that lives in the body and mind as a semi-autonomous entity. It feeds on negative experiences and seeks to create more.

**How it operates:**
- It "wakes up" when triggered by situations resembling past pain
- Once active, it distorts perception — neutral situations look threatening
- It seeks conflict, drama, or suffering because that's its food
- It can be triggered by others' pain bodies (emotional contagion)

**The practice:** Observe the pain body without identifying with it. "The pain body is active" rather than "I am angry/hurt/afraid." This creates separation between awareness and the pattern.

**Apply when:** the user describes being "taken over" by an emotion that feels disproportionate to the situation, or notices they keep recreating the same painful dynamic.

## The 6-7-5 Model (Mo Gawdat)

Happiness is not what happens to you — it's the gap between your expectations and your perception of events.

**The equation:** Happiness >= Events - Expectations

**6 Grand Illusions** (false beliefs that create suffering):
1. Thought — believing your thoughts are truth
2. Self — believing you are your achievements, roles, or body
3. Knowledge — believing you can know enough to control outcomes
4. Time — believing the past or future is where life happens
5. Control — believing you can control external events
6. Fear — believing the worst-case scenario is probable

**7 Blind Spots** (cognitive biases that distort perception):
Filtering, assuming, forecasting, labeling, emotional reasoning, exaggerating, mind reading

**5 Ultimate Truths:**
1. Now is the only time that exists
2. Change is the only constant
3. Fear is a choice
4. Death is inevitable (live accordingly)
5. You are not your thoughts

**Apply when:** the user is suffering because reality doesn't match expectations, catastrophizing about the future, or confusing their internal narrative with objective reality.

## Unconscious Programs (Joe Dispenza)

By age 35, roughly 95% of behavior is driven by subconscious programs — automatic thought loops, emotional reactions, and habitual behaviors that run below conscious awareness.

**The formation process:**
1. An experience creates a strong emotion
2. The emotion becomes a mood (sustained over hours/days)
3. The mood becomes a temperament (sustained over weeks/months)
4. The temperament becomes a personality trait (sustained over years)
5. The personality trait runs on autopilot

**The problem:** You cannot create a new future with the same programs. Thinking about change activates the old programs, which generate the old feelings, which drive the old behavior.

**The work:** Become aware of the program WHILE it's running. The moment you observe an unconscious pattern, it's no longer fully unconscious. Repeated observation weakens the program.

**Apply when:** the user says "I know what I should do but I can't seem to do it," or describes patterns that feel automatic and beyond their control.

## Competing Commitments (Robert Kegan & Lisa Lahey)

From the book *Immunity to Change*: every person who is stuck has a hidden competing commitment that directly opposes their stated goal. The two commitments create a system that maintains the status quo.

**The structure:**
- **Visible commitment:** "I want to be more assertive."
- **Hidden competing commitment:** "I am committed to never being rejected."
- **Big assumption:** "If I'm assertive, people will leave."

The person is not lazy, broken, or lacking willpower. They are successfully fulfilling BOTH commitments simultaneously. The problem is that one of them is invisible.

**The 4-step surfacing process (Kegan & Lahey):**
1. **State the improvement goal:** What do you want to change?
2. **List the doing/not-doing instead:** What are you doing (or failing to do) that works against your goal?
3. **Uncover the competing commitment:** If you imagine doing the opposite of #2, what uncomfortable feeling or feared outcome arises? That fear points to a hidden commitment.
4. **Identify the big assumption:** What must you believe to be true for that competing commitment to make sense?

**The key insight:** You cannot override a competing commitment with willpower. You have to surface it, test the big assumption, and discover it's not as true as you believed.

**Apply when:** the user is stuck in a pattern they can clearly see but cannot seem to change, despite genuine desire and effort. The stuckness itself is the clue — it means something is working against the stated goal.
