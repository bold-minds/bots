# Relationships

Use when the user is struggling with a relationship — romantic, family, friendship, or professional. Conflict, disconnection, communication breakdowns, boundary issues, or trying to understand another person's behavior.

---

## The 5 Love Languages (Gary Chapman)

People give and receive love in different languages. Most relationship frustration comes from speaking your own language instead of your partner's.

**The 5 Languages:**
1. **Words of Affirmation:** Verbal compliments, encouragement, appreciation. "I'm proud of you." "You did a great job."
2. **Quality Time:** Undivided attention. Not being in the same room — being fully present. Phones down, eyes up.
3. **Receiving Gifts:** Thoughtful tokens that show "I was thinking about you." Not about expense — about the thought.
4. **Acts of Service:** Doing things that make their life easier. Cooking dinner. Handling a task they dread.
5. **Physical Touch:** Hugs, hand-holding, a hand on the shoulder. Physical presence and connection.

**The key insight:** You naturally speak your own love language to others. If your language is Acts of Service and theirs is Words of Affirmation, you'll exhaust yourself doing things for them while they feel unloved because you never say it.

**Apply when:** the user feels unappreciated despite effort, or their partner says "you don't love me" when the user knows they do. The issue is likely a language mismatch.

## The Love Tank (Gary Chapman)

Every person has an emotional love tank. When it's full, they feel secure, generous, and resilient. When it's empty, they become defensive, needy, or withdrawn.

**The principle:** Most relationship conflicts are not about the topic of the argument. They're about an empty love tank. The dishes, the schedule, the in-laws — these are proxy wars for "I don't feel loved."

**The practice:**
- Learn to read tank levels — yours and theirs
- Fill the tank in their language, not yours
- When conflict escalates, ask: "Is this really about [topic], or is your tank low?"
- A full tank makes almost everything negotiable. An empty tank makes everything a fight.

**Apply when:** the user is in recurring conflict that seems disproportionate to the issue, or feels like they're walking on eggshells. The underlying issue may be tank levels, not the surface topic.

## The 5 Apology Languages (Gary Chapman)

Just as people have different love languages, they have different apology languages. An apology in the wrong language doesn't register.

**The 5 Apology Languages:**
1. **Expressing Regret:** "I'm sorry." (The emotional component — showing you understand the pain caused.)
2. **Accepting Responsibility:** "I was wrong." (No excuses, no justifications, no "but.")
3. **Making Restitution:** "How can I make it right?" (Action to repair the damage.)
4. **Genuinely Repenting:** "I will change." (A plan to not repeat the behavior.)
5. **Requesting Forgiveness:** "Will you forgive me?" (Putting the power in their hands.)

**The key insight:** Some people need to hear "I was wrong" and nothing else matters until they do. Others need to see changed behavior. Saying "I'm sorry" to someone who needs "Here's my plan to change" leaves the wound open.

**Apply when:** the user has apologized but the other person isn't moving past it, or feels like their apologies "never work." They may be apologizing in the wrong language.

## BRAVING — The Anatomy of Trust (Brene Brown)

Trust is not a single thing you either have or don't. It's built (and broken) in specific, observable ways.

**B.R.A.V.I.N.G.:**
- **B — Boundaries:** You respect my boundaries, and when unclear, you ask. I do the same.
- **R — Reliability:** You do what you say you'll do. Consistently, not just when it's convenient.
- **A — Accountability:** You own your mistakes, apologize, and make amends. I do the same.
- **V — Vault:** What I share with you stays with you. You don't share my stories or others' stories with me.
- **I — Integrity:** You choose courage over comfort. You practice your values, not just profess them.
- **N — Non-judgment:** I can ask for help without being judged. You can struggle in front of me.
- **G — Generosity:** You extend the most generous interpretation of my intentions, words, and actions.

**Apply when:** the user has a trust issue in a relationship — either they don't trust someone or someone doesn't trust them. Use BRAVING to diagnose which specific element is broken instead of treating "trust" as a monolith.

## How to Win Friends and Influence People (Dale Carnegie)

Timeless principles for building relationships through genuine interest in others.

**Core principles:**
1. **Don't criticize, condemn, or complain.** Criticism puts people on the defensive and achieves nothing lasting.
2. **Give honest and sincere appreciation.** Not flattery — genuine recognition of what someone has done well.
3. **Become genuinely interested in other people.** Ask about their world. Remember details. Follow up.
4. **Be a good listener. Encourage others to talk about themselves.** The most interesting person in the room is the one who's most interested.
5. **Talk in terms of the other person's interests.** Frame your requests in terms of what they want, not what you want.
6. **Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely.** Everyone wants to feel significant.

**The meta-principle:** You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than in two years of trying to get people interested in you.

**Apply when:** the user is struggling to build relationships, feels isolated, has trouble connecting, or is in conflict because they're leading with their own needs. The shift is from self-focus to other-focus.

## Givers, Takers, and Matchers (Adam Grant)

From *Give and Take*: people have fundamentally different approaches to reciprocity, and understanding this explains a lot about relationship dynamics.

**The three styles:**
- **Givers:** Default to helping, sharing, and mentoring without keeping score.
- **Takers:** Default to extracting value, claiming credit, and prioritizing self-interest.
- **Matchers:** Keep a mental ledger. Help those who help them. Punish those who take.

**The paradox of givers:** Givers are found at both the top AND bottom of success metrics. The difference:
- **Failed givers** give without boundaries — they're exploited by takers and burn out.
- **Successful givers** are "otherish" — they give strategically, set boundaries, and choose who to invest in.

**The key insight:** Being a giver is not the problem. Being a giver without boundaries is the problem. Successful givers give AND protect their energy.

**Apply when:** the user is burning out from giving too much, keeps encountering people who take advantage, or can't figure out why their generosity isn't reciprocated. Help them identify the takers and build boundaries without becoming a matcher.

## Empathic Listening (Stephen Covey)

From Habit 5 of *The 7 Habits*: "Seek first to understand, then to be understood." Most people listen with the intent to reply, not to understand.

**The 5 levels of listening:**
1. **Ignoring:** Not listening at all.
2. **Pretending:** "Uh-huh, right, sure."
3. **Selective:** Hearing only the parts you want to hear.
4. **Attentive:** Paying attention to the words.
5. **Empathic:** Listening to understand the feeling AND the meaning behind the words.

**Empathic listening in practice:**
- Reflect content: "So what you're saying is..."
- Reflect feeling: "You're feeling frustrated because..."
- Reflect content AND feeling: "You're feeling [emotion] because [situation]"
- Do NOT advise, probe, interpret, or evaluate until they feel understood

**The key insight:** People do not open up until they feel understood. If you jump to advice or your own perspective too early, they shut down. Understanding must come first.

**Apply when:** the user is in a conflict where both sides feel unheard, or they're trying to get through to someone who seems closed off. The missing step is usually genuine understanding before problem-solving.
