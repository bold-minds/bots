# Money and Resources

Use when the user is dealing with money stress, financial decisions, spending patterns, debt anxiety, wealth-building questions, or the emotional side of money management.

---

## The Total Money Makeover (Dave Ramsey)

A behavior-based financial system built on intensity, simplicity, and sequential focus.

**The 7 Baby Steps:**
1. Save $1,000 starter emergency fund (fast — sell things, work extra, create urgency)
2. Pay off all debt (except house) using the Debt Snowball — smallest balance first
3. Save 3-6 months expenses in a fully funded emergency fund
4. Invest 15% of household income for retirement
5. Save for children's college
6. Pay off the home early
7. Build wealth and give generously

**The Debt Snowball:** List debts smallest to largest (ignore interest rates). Pay minimums on everything except the smallest. Attack the smallest with every extra dollar. When it's gone, roll that payment to the next. The quick wins build momentum.

**Key principles:**
- "Act your wage." Don't live a lifestyle you haven't earned yet.
- The borrower is slave to the lender. Debt eliminates options.
- "If you will live like no one else, later you can live like no one else."

**Apply when:** the user is overwhelmed by debt, needs a concrete step-by-step plan, or is looking for someone to tell them "stop borrowing money." Ramsey is the framework for intensity and behavioral change around debt.

## I Will Teach You to Be Rich (Ramit Sethi)

The framework for people who want to be smart about money without being miserable about it.

**The Conscious Spending Plan:**
1. **Fixed costs:** 50-60% (rent, utilities, insurance, debt minimums)
2. **Investments:** 10%+ (automated into index funds, 401k, Roth IRA)
3. **Savings:** 5-10% (emergency fund, vacations, big purchases)
4. **Guilt-free spending:** 20-35% (whatever you want, no justification)

**Key principles:**

- **Spend extravagantly on things you love. Cut mercilessly on things you don't.** Not every dollar matters equally. The $3 latte is irrelevant if you're saving 20% of income.

- **Automate everything.** Willpower is unreliable. Set up automatic transfers so saving and investing happen without decisions.

- **Focus on the Big Wins:** Negotiating salary, automating investments, and choosing the right accounts matter more than cutting small expenses.

- **The $30,000 question:** Instead of asking "how do I save $3 a day?" ask "how do I earn $30,000 more a year?" Offense beats defense.

**Apply when:** the user wants to build wealth without living on beans and rice, is overwhelmed by traditional budgeting, or needs permission to spend on things they love while still being responsible.

## The Psychology of Money (Morgan Housel)

Money is not a math problem. It's a behavior problem shaped by personal history, emotions, and narrative.

**Key lessons:**

- **No one's crazy.** Your money decisions make sense given your unique experiences. Someone who grew up in poverty and someone who grew up wealthy will make different — but internally logical — financial choices.

- **Wealth is what you don't see.** The car, the house, the watch — that's spending, not wealth. Wealth is the money you didn't spend. It's options. It's freedom.

- **Getting wealthy vs. staying wealthy:** Getting wealthy requires optimism and risk-taking. Staying wealthy requires humility, frugality, and paranoia. They are different skills.

- **Reasonable > Rational:** You don't need the mathematically optimal portfolio. You need a portfolio you won't abandon when the market drops 30%. The best strategy is the one you can stick with.

- **Room for error:** The most important financial skill is getting the goalpost to stop moving. Define "enough" and stop.

- **Compounding:** The entire game. Small, consistent investments over long periods produce extraordinary results. The hard part is not finding the right investment — it's being patient enough.

**Apply when:** the user is making emotionally driven financial decisions, comparing themselves to others' visible spending, struggling with the concept of "enough," or needs perspective on long-term wealth building vs. short-term optimization.

## Thinking, Fast and Slow — Money Biases (Daniel Kahneman)

Specific cognitive biases that sabotage financial decisions.

**Loss aversion:** Losing $100 feels about twice as bad as gaining $100 feels good. This causes people to:
- Hold losing investments too long (selling would "make the loss real")
- Avoid reasonable risks that have positive expected value
- Make irrational insurance decisions (overinsuring against small, unlikely losses)

**Mental accounting:** Treating money differently depending on its source or intended use. A tax refund feels like "free money" even though it's your own money that was over-withheld. A bonus feels different from salary even though they're both income.

**Anchoring:** The price you paid for something (or the first price you see) becomes a reference point that distorts all future evaluation. "It's 50% off" feels like a deal even if the original price was inflated.

**Present bias:** A dollar today feels worth much more than a dollar next year. This is why saving is hard and debt is easy — the spending is immediate, the consequences are future.

**Apply when:** the user is making a financial decision and a specific bias is visibly driving the reasoning. Name the bias, explain the mechanism, and help them see the decision without the distortion.
