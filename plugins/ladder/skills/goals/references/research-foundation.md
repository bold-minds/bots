# Goal Framework Research Foundation

The three-layer goal structure (Needle → Commitments → When-Thens) synthesizes evidence from multiple research traditions. This reference provides the basis for why each layer works, so the skill can explain the reasoning to the user when needed.

## Layer 1: The Needle (Lag Measure)

Based on **4DX (4 Disciplines of Execution)** — McChesney, Covey, Huling. The distinction between lag measures (outcomes you want) and lead measures (behaviors that drive outcomes) is the core insight. Track the lag measure to know if things are working. Act on the lead measures to make things work.

Also draws on **Locke & Latham's goal-setting theory** — specific, difficult goals outperform vague "do your best" goals across 1000+ studies. The needle must be specific enough to answer "did it move?" weekly.

## Layer 2: Commitments (Lead Measures)

Based on **pre-commitment / Ulysses contracts** — Schelling (Nobel laureate). Making decisions in a calm, rational state that bind your future self when temptation arrives. The commitment must have external structure because willpower alone is unreliable.

**Accountability research** — sharing goals as commitments with check-ins increases follow-through (distinct from sharing goals as announcements, which can reduce effort via premature identity satisfaction — Gollwitzer, 2009).

**Loss aversion / stakes** — Kahneman & Tversky's prospect theory. Losses are ~2x as psychologically powerful as equivalent gains. Financial stakes (stickK model) and social stakes (people counting on you) leverage this.

**Binary tracking** from **4DX** — the scoreboard must be simple. Kept or broken. No gray zone.

## Layer 3: When-Thens (Implementation Intentions)

Based on **Peter Gollwitzer's implementation intentions** research — medium-to-large effect size (d = 0.65) across 94 studies. "When X happens, I will do Y" creates strategic automaticity — the behavior becomes quasi-automatic, bypassing the need for in-the-moment willpower.

Most effective for people who struggle with self-regulation in specific domains.

## The WOOP Addition

Based on **Gabriele Oettingen's mental contrasting** — positive visualization alone undermines goal achievement by giving the brain premature reward. WOOP (Wish, Outcome, Obstacle, Plan) combines positive visualization with obstacle identification, creating energization rather than complacency.

The obstacle step is critical: it forces naming the *internal* barrier (not "I don't have time" but the real feeling or belief underneath). This self-honesty is what makes the subsequent implementation intention targeted.

## Self-Determination Theory Context

**Deci & Ryan** — activities that satisfy autonomy, competence, and relatedness are intrinsically motivating. When designing non-work goals, include skill development (competence), social connection (relatedness), and personal choice (autonomy) — not just obligation. Goals that feel like chores without satisfying these needs are harder to sustain.

## Common Anti-Patterns

These are traps that undermine goal systems. The system should watch for them during goal creation:

- **Goal announcements without accountability structure** — announcing a goal publicly can create premature identity satisfaction (Gollwitzer, 2009), reducing the drive to actually do it. Goals need check-ins, not just announcements.
- **Positive visualization without mental contrasting** — fantasizing about the outcome without naming the obstacle reduces effort (Oettingen). Always pair the desired outcome with the internal obstacle.
- **Willpower-based plans** — any plan that depends entirely on the user choosing correctly in the moment. External structure is the solution.
- **Vague tracking** — if the metric requires judgment to evaluate, it will be judged favorably. Binary only.
